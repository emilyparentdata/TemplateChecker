How Does Day Care Impact Children?

What the research really says.

Emily Oster

Child care choices always rank high in parent discussion and rightly so. Does day care generate behavior problems? Is a nanny dangerous? There are often but not always from the political right, for more women to stay home with their kids. I have heard, more than once, the view that "Of course it's the case that it is bad for your children." I'm not.

There are many aspects to these questions too many for one post. But one claim that has come up in more places lately is the focus on "research says" that day care is bad for children. More on that in a second. It's followed by a discussion of one particular study, from Canada. Instagram DMs, twitter; they all appear with frequency — "Is this true?" or, more commonly, "This made me feel terrible."

Here, I'll talk about that one study but I want to start by talking at more length about the bigger picture in this literature. It's long and technical, and... that's kind of my point. Let's go!

Summarizing the literature

No study lives alone. Especially not on a topic people care about as much as they care about this one. There are many, many studies on child care and child childhood education on childhood outcomes.

The simplest way to look at the impact of child care on child outcomes: first, the child care choices and later child outcomes, and compare.

The largest and best-known study to do this is one out of the National Institute of Child Health and Human Development. This will just call the NICHD study. I talk at length about this in Chapter 4 (where I go over how to think about child care choices more holistically). This particular study 1000 children over time and was designed to evaluate the impact of various types of child care on child development.

The authors do a number of things with this data. One is that comparison of child care center-based child care centers to those with no center experience — family care and babysitters. When they do this, they find that use of child care is associated with somewhat higher cognitive outcomes for one year olds and slightly higher ages. By six months and 24 months. Overall — as in, if the child was enrolled for the entire period — the impact on cognitive outcomes is positive. They find slightly negative outcomes on behavior; see also for later-age outcomes. Both sets of effects are, again, fairly small and results are a bit mixed.

The study also finds that higher-quality day care — as measured at six locations provide a better environment — is associated with better outcomes. This is not surprising. An important note is that when we say "high-quality day care" in a study like this, it doesn't mean "fancy". It's about how the adults interact, not about whether the snacks are organic.

This study has a lot to recommend it, but it's not perfect. The main concern with this study and most others in this space is to adjust for differences across families that choose different forms of child care and later child outcomes. This is a standard econometric concern.

The Bottom Line
  • Center-based child care may have slight downsides before 18 months and slight upsides after that. These impacts are small in either direction, and are likely outweighed by the other considerations that are important in your choice of child care. If you do choose center-based child care, quality matters, which means caregivers who are attentive to the kids and a safe environment.
  • I want you to know that I think you're doing a great job. My biggest frustration with so many of these panic headlines and reels is that they take people who were happy with how things are going and make them feel like bad parents. You are a good parent. Period.
